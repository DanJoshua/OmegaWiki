## Statement

In open-ended LLM-agent task settings, agents equipped with **human-curated** skill libraries outperform agents with skills generated by zero-shot self-generation. The gap is large enough that smaller models with curated skills can outperform larger models without them.

## Evidence summary

- SkillsBench (as reported in the SoK paper): +16.2 pp average pass-rate gain with curated skills; -1.3 pp average pass-rate change with self-generated skills.
- Only 1 of 5 self-generation configurations on SkillsBench showed any improvement.
- Domain breakdown: gains are largest where pretraining is procedurally sparse (healthcare +51.9 pp, manufacturing +41.9 pp); modest in code/math (SWE +4.5 pp, math +6.0 pp).
- Constrained-environment counter-evidence: Voyager (Minecraft) and Eureka (robotics) demonstrate that self-generation *can* succeed when paired with deterministic, automated execution-verification — pointing to verification, not generation, as the bottleneck.

## Conditions and scope

- Open-ended task domains without automated verification harnesses.
- Zero-shot self-generation without iterative quality gates.
- Single-benchmark anchor (SkillsBench, 86 tasks); generalization to other benchmarks is untested in the paper.

## Counter-evidence

- Voyager (Minecraft) and Eureka (robot reward design) report successful self-generated skills, but with strong domain constraints and verification loops.
- The +16.2 pp / -1.3 pp numbers come from a single benchmark and a single source, awaiting independent replication.

## Open questions

- Does the curated > self-generated gap close when self-generation is paired with regression-style verification before library admission?
- How much of the gap is benchmark-specific vs general?
- Can hybrid pipelines (self-generate + human review) recover most of the curated gain at lower cost?
